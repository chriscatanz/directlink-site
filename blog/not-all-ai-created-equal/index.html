Not All AI is Created Equal

December 12, 2024 Technology

Artificial intelligence has become the most talked-about technology in banking. From customer service chatbots to fraud detection systems, financial institutions are racing to implement AI solutions that promise efficiency gains and competitive advantages. But here is the uncomfortable truth that many vendors would prefer you not consider: not all AI is created equal, and the differences matter enormously in a regulated industry like banking.

The Allure of Generic AI

When ChatGPT burst onto the scene, it captured the imagination of business leaders everywhere. The technology seemed almost magical in its ability to generate human-like responses, summarize documents, and answer complex questions. Naturally, many banks began asking: can we use this to serve our customers better?

The answer is nuanced. Generic large language models are remarkable tools, but they were not built with banking in mind. They were trained on vast swaths of internet data, optimized for general conversation, and designed to be helpful across thousands of use cases. That breadth comes at a cost: they lack the specialized knowledge, guardrails, and compliance awareness that financial services demand.

The Hallucination Problem

One of the most significant risks with generic AI is hallucination, the tendency for language models to generate confident-sounding but completely false information. In a consumer application, a hallucinated fact might be a minor inconvenience. In banking, it could mean providing incorrect information about loan terms, misrepresenting account balances, or offering advice that violates regulations.

Purpose-built banking AI addresses this challenge through multiple layers of protection. These systems are trained on verified financial data, constrained to specific domains, and designed to acknowledge uncertainty rather than fabricate answers. When a banking AI does not know something, it should say so clearly rather than improvise a plausible-sounding response.

Compliance Is Not Optional

Banks operate under intense regulatory scrutiny. Every customer interaction, every piece of advice, and every decision must be defensible to examiners. Generic AI tools were never designed with this reality in mind.

Consider what happens when a regulator asks your bank to explain why a particular response was given to a customer. With many off-the-shelf AI solutions, you simply cannot provide that answer. The model is a black box, and its reasoning is opaque. This is not acceptable in an environment where transparency and accountability are fundamental requirements.

Purpose-built banking AI must include comprehensive audit trails, logging every interaction with enough detail to reconstruct the decision-making process. Examiner-ready logs should capture not just what the AI said, but what information it accessed, what rules it applied, and how it arrived at its response.

Data Security Demands Vigilance

Banking data is among the most sensitive information that exists. Customer financial records, account numbers, transaction histories: these require the highest levels of protection. When evaluating AI solutions, banks must understand exactly where their data travels, who can access it, and how it is protected.

Many generic AI services process data through shared infrastructure, sometimes across international boundaries. For banks subject to strict data residency requirements or concerned about third-party access, this presents unacceptable risk. Purpose-built banking AI should offer deployment options that keep sensitive data within controlled environments.

Questions Every Bank Should Ask AI Vendors

Before implementing any AI solution, banks should conduct thorough due diligence. Here are critical questions to ask every vendor:

1. How does your system prevent and detect hallucinations? Look for specific technical approaches, not vague assurances.

2. What audit trail capabilities do you provide? Can you demonstrate examiner-ready logging that captures the full context of each interaction?

3. Where is our data processed and stored? Understand the complete data flow, including any third-party services involved.

4. How do you handle regulatory compliance requirements? The vendor should understand banking regulations and demonstrate how their system supports compliance.

5. What happens when the AI encounters something outside its training? Graceful handling of edge cases is essential.

6. How do you manage model updates and version control? Banks need predictability and the ability to validate changes before deployment.

7. Can you provide references from other regulated financial institutions? Experience in banking is not optional for a banking AI vendor.
